The following is an email from a journeyman PCC employee to CEO Cate Hardy


PCC: Be A Part Of Something Bigger

Dear Cate,

I am writing to you as a longtime PCC employee, wishing to offer

some perspective on the dynamics I see taking place in our company. I

am writing not only on behalf of myself, but also for my incredible

coworkers at Greenlake Aurora (both past and present), and the

entire community of PCC employees. I would like to thank you in

advance for taking the time and attention to hear what I have to say.


Although I only recently celebrated my ten-year anniversary, I

first started at PCC twenty years ago. I was hired in the produce

department at the old Ravenna store, I worked night crew at old

Fremont, grand-opened the Edmonds store in 2008, and have been at

Greenlake Aurora since 2013. I have left PCC several times to do

other things, but I have returned each time because there was

something special about us. Our stores were warm and inviting, the

staff were genuinely happy to be at work, and the customers were

dedicated, loyal, and grateful. I have worked many places over the

years, but PCC was always the best.

Over the last several years, but especially since the rebranding,

I have become increasingly concerned about the direction in which our

company is headed. Every company goes through changes, and I have seen

PCC go through many, but it is only recently that these changes have

had an overwhelmingly negative effect on our staff, our customers, and

our co-op. I have seen the company I loved transformed into something

I barely recognize, and it cuts me to the the core every day. I know I

am not alone in this.

It's easy to merely criticize, while solutions require thought

and effort; I do not wish to merely criticize, nor do I believe that I

have all the solutions. There are a number of deeply interconnected

issues facing us, but I would like to address several key issues

individually and offer opportunities for us to do to better. Using

Greenlake Aurora as an example, but knowing these issues to be

prevalent in many of our stores, I write this because I cannot bear to

see the company I care about heading down the wrong path, and I write

in the knowledge that I am not alone in these views. I believe we

can do better.


ISSUE 1: STAFF MORALE IS LOWER THAN EVER.

Last year, at the all-staff meetings, you opened each session by

telling us the responses to two questions from the employee survey:

"Do you feel supported by management," and "Do you trust the

leadership of the company?" The answer to each was an overwhelming

"No." In the intervening year since receiving this alarming feedback

from the majority of the staff, the response has been continued cuts

to staffing, continued changes to our stores, and an increasingly

hierarchical decision-making process which is hurting our staff, our

customers, and our business.

When I started at Greenlake in 2013, I had never been in a store

which was so vibrantly alive; humming with business all day long,

staff in every aisle, three or four check stands running most of the

time. There were always seven or eight people in the break room, and

the entire staff was tighter-knit than anything I had experienced

before. There was a magic to working (and shopping) in our store.

Yes, it was often crowded, and we all said "excuse me," more often,

but it was alive and pulsing, like a beating heart. We were filled

with long-time customers shopping for the family, neighborhood folks,

and people who went out of their way to shop at PCC. Every day we could

feel a sense of accomplishment, having done our jobs well and served

our customers to the best of our abilities.

In the five years since Greenlake Village opened, we have seen

our sales and customer count plummet, our staffing drastically

reduced, and have been forced to adopt an increasing number of

policies and procedures which make our job more difficult and less

rewarding. The staff are stressed, doing two or three jobs apiece, and the

customers feel the shift in the store. The employees work (and the

customers shop)

in a chaotic atmosphere, where people are constantly pulled out of

their departments to do other jobs. Customers have a hard time finding

staff on the floor to answer

questions, and we struggle to make it through our shifts with our

sanity intact. We have lost good employees because of this taxing,

grinding environment, and we are sure to lose more as time goes on.


OPPORTUNITY 1: FULL STAFFING MUST BE A PRIORITY

Cuts to staffing affect our customers, our sales, and our

employees. In years past I have seen labor hours go down due to

seasonal shopping trends, and the result has always been the same:

sales go down so we cut labor, but decreased staff on the sales floor

affects customer satisfaction, which drives our sales down further; I

have seen this pattern play out over and over again, and now it seems

to have solidified into baseline policy. One of the things that used

to set us apart from every other grocery store in Seattle is that we

were better staffed than anyone else, and there was always an employee

at hand to ask for help or information. If we want to be a company

that does things differently and better than the other guys, we need

to start here. An employee who feels left out in the wind may stick it

out because they need a job, but a customer who feels left out in the

wind will find a different place to shop.


ISSUE 2: GROWING ANIMOSITY BETWEEN THE STAFF AND THE OFFICE

This is perhaps the most concerning problem facing PCC. We were

started as a co-op and thrived for 50 years under a business model

which placed an emphasis on cooperation and democratic decision

making, but in the last few years we have seen that shift to an

increasingly hierarchical and corporate methodology. The decision

making process has become almost entirely top-down, with little or no

input from the floor staff who actually do the work and interact with

the customers. We used to function with a high degree of autonomy,

where each department and store was able (in large part) to make

decisions based on what worked for their location and their customers,

and we relied on the expertise of our staff to make those decisions

wisely. Whether that was designing our own end caps, letting the deli

decide what dishes to make for the hot bar, or the arrangement of

items on our shelves, we had the ability to be creative and solve

problems on our own, based on what made sense for our store. Now, we

have had our autonomy effectively stripped away, and are forced to

swallow policies and procedures which make very little sense and more

often than not make our jobs more difficult and less satisfying.

In addition, we have seen the office staff continue to grow while

the floor staff continues to shrink. We work everyday in a chaotic and

stressful environment while the office staff heads to a luxurious (and

no doubt expensive) waterfront building--this is antithetical to co-op

values. For the few to reap the rewards from the work of the many is

not only unethical, it is fiscally irresponsible. Reducing staff to

pay for things we don't need (and should not have) is like cutting the

roots out of a tree to build a treehouse, and a tree with no roots

cannot stand.


OPPORTUNITY 2: GIVE THE POWER BACK TO THE PEOPLE

We need to return to true democratic and co-operative decision

making, where solutions and ideas come from the ground up. Staff and

stores need to have the freedom to make logical decisions for

themselves, whether that be end cap arrangement, deli recipes, shelf

sets, or the management of discontinued items. Something that works

well in Issaquah may

not work at Greenlake or Fremont, and it makes no sense to impose

uniformity on the stores. Each location should be a unique part of a

larger whole, reflecting the communities they serve.

The continual expansion of the office needs to stop, and the

waterfront office needs to go. The best way to ensure that we are

making decisions which are good for the stores and good for the

company is to integrate a large number of the office staff back into

the stores, and into the departments they serve. No one loses their

job, we increase staffing in the stores, and problems and solutions

will come from first-hand experience and face-to-face interactions

with customers and co-workers, not from a spreadsheet. The remaining

office staff who truly need to be in an office to do their jobs

effectively can move to a more modest, affordable, and central

location. Instead of "Us and Them," we can truly be "Us."


ISSUE 3: CONSTANT CHANGES ARE HURTING STAFF AND CUSTOMERS ALIKE

The number one thing I heard from customers in the lead-up to the

rebranding and in the weeks and months afterwards was "I don't

recognize PCC anymore." The onslaught of changes--to our signage, the

look of our stores, the policies--have all negatively impacted staff

morale and customer experience, and have stripped away many of the

things which made PCC a unique place to work and shop.

Using the signage as a concrete example, our old signs were

colorful, attractive, informative, and easy to work with. The new

signage is bland, hard to read, and a hassle to utilize. Not only are

they a pain for those of us who do POS, but I have had innumerable

customers complain that the new signs are hard to read and difficult

to decipher. I know there were some issues with the old shelf tags, but we

have treated a stubbed toe by cutting off the leg and replacing it

with an ill-fitting prosthesis. If the entire POS staff had been

consulted, I am certain we could have found a solution together.

New signage, repainting the stores, the new aprons, the new logo,

taking over the music--these are change for the sake of change, and they all take

something away rather than adding something new and better. PCC stood

out from the crowd because we looked and felt different from the other

guys, but these changes strip away that uniqueness. When they came in

and painted Greenlake white it was like someone came into my house,

stole all the artwork, and erased everything that made it such a homey

place. I have had exactly two customers tell me they liked the new

look, while I have had hundreds tell me how saddened they were. (For

one of my longtime regulars, this was the last straw--after I rang her

up she handed me her PCC card and said "Good luck." She has never been

back.) The decision not to carry calendars alienated countless

members, some of whom have been buying calendars from us for twenty

years. I could go on and on, but suffice it to say that the result of

these changes is overwhelmingly negative and is costing us business.


OPPORTUNITY 3: RESTORE THE ELEMENTS WHICH MAKE US UNIQUE

PCC is an heirloom company, and heirlooms need to be cared for.

Whether it's a tomato or an antique wardrobe, you have to nurture it,

tend to it, and emphasize the things which make it unique and

beautiful. You wouldn't cross a Brandywine with a Monsanto Beefsteak

anymore than than you would sand and spray paint a Louis XIV armoire.

It's not too late to undo many of these changes, and bring back the

things which were appreciated by staff and members alike. We have

stood for 50 years on the strength of our differences, and with care

and stewardship we can stand for many, many more.


ISSUE 4: PUSH FOR GROWTH IS UNSUSTAINABLE

I have been through new staff orientation twice, once in 1997 and

again in 2008. At each meeting, one of the first things that Mimi told

us is that we only open new stores with cash we have saved, and that

this was part of PCC's commitment to ethical stewardship of our

resources--I can't imagine that this still the case (if this is the

case, then we are putting those resources in the wrong place.) We are

stretched too thin, and I don't see how these new stores can happen

without hurting the ones we already have. We don't have the existing

staffing to support all these new locations, and increasing number of

outside hires means we will soon have a staff which is mainly

comprised of people who simply need a job rather than people who share

a vision.

Healthy growth has to happen organically and responsibly, and

that growth should be putting more of a good thing into the world. The

growth we are facing now is not healthy, it is cancerous. We are

taking resources from out existing stores to expand our footprint, but

the mark we are leaving is increasingly indistinguishable from

everyone else.


OPPORTUNITY 4: FOCUS ON EXISTING STORES FIRST

If we are going to continue to grow, that growth needs to come

about because we are doing so well that we have outgrown our current

capacity to serve our communities and our customers. We need to get

our stores back to full staffing levels, training up the next

generation of employees who will then move out into new locations and

spread the commitments and passions which have always made PCC unique

place to work and shop.


ISSUE 5: ACTIONS DON'T LINE UP WITH WORDS

The three words which come up most often in emails to the staff

are Community, Integrity, and Transparency, but those important ideals

are not reflected in our day-to day operations. These are big,

important words, and they must have action behind them or they are

just that--words. In the ways I have detailed above, and many more, we

are underserving our customers, our communities, and most importantly,

our staff.

Speaking For Myself, but knowing this to be true of many of my

coworkers, as a full-time journeyman employee I am completely and

utterly broke. We have not seen a significant raise in fifteen years,

and our jobs which were once considered high-paying now keep us just

above the poverty level. We have all seen the cost of living triple in

the last decade, while our pay remains well below a true living wage.

Every year we see more and more of our paycheck go to health care costs

and taxes, and every time our contract negotiations come around we see

management trying to give us less and less. All of us are struggling

to keep our heads above water, and many of us are on the verge of

drowning.

We have been a union shop since I was first hired in 1997, and

part of that commitment to a unionized workplace was to provide the

employees with good wages and a stable work environment--we no longer

have that. If we are going to live up to the statement that "Community

is our middle name," we need to value the work of our employees--the

community which makes this business function on a daily basis. We live

in one of the most progressive cities in the world, and I know that

people would flock to us if they knew their dollars were going to

directly support the people who serve them day after day, to provide a

livelihood for the people they know and trust. We could become the

most successful grocery store in Seattle overnight based on the

strength of this alone.


OPPORTUNITY 5: PUT THE WORDS INTO ACTION

We stand at a crossroads--as a community, as a company, and as a

country. The divide between "Us" and "Them" is sharper than ever, and

the animosities which come with it are as strong as they have ever

been. We have an opportunity before us, an opportunity to choose to do

things differently and show that it works. We have an opportunity to

show that a multi-million dollar company can redefine what it means to

be successful, that we can thrive and grow and improve the lives of

everyone involved; staff, customers, and community alike.


If we are going to embody Community, we need to pay our staff a

true living wage,

to provide a livelihood which values what we do every day. We need to bring back

the members we have lost as well as attracting new members, by showing

them that choosing PCC is not just choosing a place to buy groceries,

but it is participating in something that is bigger than themselves.

We have an opportunity to show the world that the status quo isn't the

only option available, and that better for everyone truly IS better

for everyone.

We need to increase member benefits and member ownership of

PCC--it was started as a community, and we need to strengthen the

bonds which kept us strong for 50 years. We need to make PCC a

destination for new members, but also a place where the members we have

lost will be proud to return. We need to truly inhabit the co-operative

spirit which was at the heart of this company from the beginning, and

place value in the many over the few. We need to rekindle the flame

which made PCC a shining beacon in a gray city.

We need to serve our communities, and we need to reflect the

uniqueness of the communities we serve. We are one PCC, but we serve

different neighborhoods, different customers, and different needs. A

garden thrives through diversity, but it is still one garden. Our

staff and our customers need to feel that their stores belong to them,

that they have ownership and can take pride in the place they work and

shop. We need to be a place worth choosing.


If we are going to embody Integrity, we need to stand by our

words. We need to be a company which values and engenders openness,

honesty, and co-operation. We need to promote empowerment and autonomy

within our stores and our staff, trusting them to make wise decisions.

Every ship needs a captain, but it can't sail smoothly unless the

entire crew is working together. If we trust and rely on each other,

we can weather any storm.


And if we are going to embody Transparency, we need to lay all of

our cards on the table; we need to hear the voices of the larger

community. We need to have an all-store, all-staff, all-member

meeting, with open and direct feedback from the people who work and

shop in our stores. We need to see where we stand as a business, and

we need to see how those resources are being allocated. We are built

on the strength of our staff and the support of our member-owners, and

we need to show that we value that strength and that support by

hearing what they have to say--the good and the bad. We need to know

how we can better serve our staff, our customers, and our communities,

and we need to do it together.


PCC: BE A PART OF SOMETHING BIGGER

I stole that line from our recruiting poster, and I repeat it

here intentionally. If we continue down our current path, we won't be a

part of something bigger, just something larger. We will have transformed from a community of

like-minded individuals who shared a vision into a place that sells groceries.

If our definition of success becomes simply "We make money," we will

have failed in

every way it is possible to fail. We have the chance to stand for

something else, to put our people above our profits, and to show that

it can work. We have the chance to be a force for good in the lives of

our staff, our customers, and our communities. We have a chance to be

an example, to be a catalyst for change--all that is required is that

we choose to do it. We can be the garden, we can be the tree, and we

can be the ship; we can be all of this and more. We can choose to be a

be a part of something bigger.



I welcome to opportunity to discuss this with you further, and hope

that my words are heard in the spirit in which they are offered.


Yours,


Matt Swanson
